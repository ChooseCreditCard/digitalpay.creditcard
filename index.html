Behind the Scenes of Digital Payments on Credit Cards

Many “buy now, pay later”, in-app purchases or wallet-based payments rely on traditional credit-card rails under the hood. This page breaks down how tokenization, virtual cards, subscription billing and digital-first payment flows work — and what it means for security and flexibility.

Compare digital-ready cards

What Is DigitalPay in the Context of Cards?

DigitalPay refers to payment flows that never see a physical card — where wallets, apps or merchants use virtual card numbers, tokens or stored-card data to process transactions. These flows work on top of existing credit-card networks but offer smoother online or mobile checkout experiences.

Examples include one-click checkout, subscription billing (card-on-file), digital wallets, in-app purchases and virtual cards issued instantly via apps. The user experience is optimized for speed and convenience while still relying on standard authorization and settlement rails.

How a DigitalPay Transaction Flows End-to-End

When you pay online or in-app, your wallet or the merchant’s payment gateway sends a tokenized card number or encrypted card-on-file identifier. The request travels through a payment gateway and acquirer, reaches the card network, and then your issuer authorizes, clears and settles the transaction like a normal card payment.

Throughout the process, tokenization and encryption protect your real card credentials. Virtual cards or tokens can be rotated, revoked or limited, reducing the risk of fraud if a merchant or service is compromised.

Virtual Cards, Tokens and Card-on-File Billing

Virtual cards are card numbers created digitally inside an app. They can be used immediately for online or in-app purchases, often before the plastic arrives — or even without a plastic card ever being issued.

Card-on-file billing lets subscriptions, recurring charges or merchant-initiated payments occur without re-entering details each time. The merchant retains a token, not the real card number, enhancing security while enabling automated billing.

Security, Fraud Controls and Tokenisation Benefits

Because transactions use tokens instead of the actual card number, merchants don’t store reusable sensitive data. If a merchant’s database is compromised, the leak affects only tokens, which can be revoked without replacing the underlying card.

Issuers and wallets can also enforce controls: per-merchant limits, one-time virtual cards, real-time spend alerts and instant card freezes — features that are harder to retrofit with legacy plastic-card systems.

Considerations: Flexibility, Recurring Fees & Merchant Risk

While digital payments are convenient, they rely on merchant compliance: tokens must be stored securely, billed properly and renewed when cards are reissued. Issues such as failed token updates can lead to declined payments or subscription interruptions.

Also, some merchants may charge different fees when processing tokens or offer less favourable FX or billing terms. It pays to monitor your digital-card usage and treat virtual cards carefully — just like physical ones.
